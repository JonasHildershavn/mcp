### AI Models Tackle Unsolved Mathematical Problems from Erdos Collection
**Why it matters:** If verified, this represents a significant milestone in AI's ability to conduct genuine mathematical research rather than merely solving well-defined problems, though important caveats remain about the nature and originality of these solutions.

Reports emerged in early January 2026 that frontier AI models, including OpenAI's GPT-5.2, have begun solving previously unsolved mathematical problems from the legendary collection of conjectures by Hungarian mathematician Paul Erdos. Cambridge student AcerFur announced on January 7 that GPT-5.2 had "autonomously" solved Erdos problem #728, with the proof formalized in the Lean verification language and accepted by Fields Medalist Terence Tao.

However, significant caveats accompany these claims. The mathematical community notes that many solved problems involve interpretations of ambiguous original statements rather than breakthroughs on clearly-defined conjectures. Some solutions appear heavily inspired by previous work from mathematicians like Carl Pomerance. The distinction matters: AI currently excels at structured, competition-level mathematics (77% success on Olympiad tasks) but struggles with genuine research requiring novel insights (25% on research tasks). This echoes October 2025 controversies when GPT-5 was found to have retrieved existing solutions from web crawls rather than generating original proofs.

### DeepSeek Introduces mHC Architecture for Stable Large-Scale Training
**Why it matters:** This architectural innovation addresses fundamental stability challenges in training massive AI models, potentially enabling more efficient development of trillion-parameter systems.

DeepSeek, the Chinese AI research lab, published a significant paper on January 1, 2026, introducing Manifold-Constrained Hyper-Connections (mHC), a novel architecture for training large language models. Co-authored by founder Liang Wenfeng, the research proposes rethinking the fundamental architecture used for foundational AI models by incorporating mathematical manifolds to maintain gradient stability as information flows between layers.

The team of 19 DeepSeek researchers tested mHC on models with 3 billion, 9 billion, and 27 billion parameters, finding it scaled effectively while adding only 6.27% hardware overhead. The mHC-powered models demonstrated superior performance across eight different AI benchmarks compared to standard architectures. Professor Quan Long of Hong Kong University of Science and Technology called the findings "very significant for transformer architecture made for LLMs." The work represents China's continued push to develop more efficient training methods that can achieve comparable results with fewer computational resources.

### NVIDIA Launches Rubin Platform: Six New Chips for Next-Gen AI
**Why it matters:** NVIDIA's new platform promises 10x reduction in inference costs and 4x fewer GPUs for training, potentially democratizing access to cutting-edge AI infrastructure while maintaining NVIDIA's dominant market position.

At CES 2026 on January 5, NVIDIA unveiled the Rubin platform, comprising six new chips designed to deliver an AI supercomputer: the Vera CPU, Rubin GPU, NVLink 6 Switch, ConnectX-9 SuperNIC, BlueField-4 DPU, and Spectrum-6 Ethernet Switch. The platform delivers up to 10x reduction in inference token cost and 4x reduction in the number of GPUs required to train mixture-of-experts models compared to NVIDIA's Blackwell platform.

The Rubin platform is already in full production, with Rubin-based products available from partners in the second half of 2026. Major cloud providers including AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft Azure, and Oracle Cloud Infrastructure have committed to deploying Vera Rubin-based instances. Notably, Microsoft's next-generation Fairwater AI superfactories will feature NVIDIA Vera Rubin NVL72 rack-scale systems, scaling to hundreds of thousands of NVIDIA Vera Rubin Superchips. The announcement solidifies NVIDIA's continued dominance in AI hardware while addressing the critical need for more cost-effective inference and training infrastructure.

## Policy & Regulation

### Trump Executive Order Challenges State AI Laws

President Trump's December 11, 2025 executive order titled "Ensuring a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence" is creating significant uncertainty for state AI laws that took effect on January 1, 2026. The executive order proposes to establish a uniform federal policy framework for AI that preempts state AI laws deemed inconsistent with that policy.

Key provisions include directing the Attorney General to establish an AI litigation task force to challenge state AI laws, requiring the Secretary of Commerce to publish by March 11, 2026, an evaluation identifying burdensome state AI laws, and threatening federal funding restrictions for states with certain AI laws. However, the executive order does not presently invalidate any state or local AI laws, which remain enforceable unless blocked by courts or preempted by Congress.

Despite the federal uncertainty, California's Transparency in Frontier Artificial Intelligence Act (TFAIA), Texas's Responsible Artificial Intelligence Governance Act (RAIGA), and 38 other state AI laws addressing topics like deepfakes in elections and medical information regulation took effect on January 1, 2026. Colorado postponed implementation of its AI Act from February 1 to June 30, 2026.
